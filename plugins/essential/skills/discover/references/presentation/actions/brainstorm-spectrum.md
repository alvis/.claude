# Brainstorm spectrum direction

Use this direction when the problem is understood but the solution space needs
deliberate breadth from the cheapest probe to the most ambitious intervention.
It should expose adjacent intervention surfaces the user may not have known to
ask about, while keeping every idea tied to evidence and a learnable question.

## Structural fidelity

1. Anchor the invariant outcome, current failure signal, audience, and evidence
   boundary. State where the code, research, or observations were inspected.
2. Offer enough breadth to reveal the shape of the space—normally eight to
   twelve grounded ideas rather than three or four generic suggestions.
3. Arrange the ideas along a meaningful axis such as time horizon, effort,
   reversibility, organizational reach, or product-surface change. Label the
   transitions between quick wiring, new UI, lifecycle change, and structural
   bet when those distinctions matter.
4. For every idea, show the intervention, its evidence anchor, what it tests,
   expected signal, and a realistic cost or reach indicator. A feature name by
   itself is not a brainstorm artifact.
5. Use an action-appropriate composition: a time-horizon rail, uneven stacked
   interventions, or grouped opportunity bands may communicate breadth better
   than a symmetrical card grid.
6. Show assumptions or evidence that could remove whole regions of the
   spectrum, and identify the cheapest probe that can discriminate between
   competing explanations.
7. Let the user select several resonant ideas across different horizons and add
   a missing possibility. Carry selections, annotations, evidence gaps, and
   untested assumptions into one prompt.

## Directional, not prescriptive

The checked-in example demonstrates credible density and time-horizon breadth;
it is not a requirement to produce ten cards or the same grouping. Add, remove,
or reshape components according to the problem. Do not force symmetrical cards
when ideas need different amounts of evidence or explanation.

## Interaction instructions

- Make every spectrum region, assumption region, selection region, and prompt
  annotatable.
- Put multi-select reactions near the ideas they describe so selections retain
  their horizon and evidence context.
- Use checkboxes when several probes may survive together.
- Keep recommendation badges provisional until the user interacts.
- Do not turn selected probes into a final architecture decision.
- Keep exactly one folded, live prompt for replying to the LLM coder.
